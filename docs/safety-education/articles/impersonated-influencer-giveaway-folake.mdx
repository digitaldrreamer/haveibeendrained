## What Happened

### Step 1: The Fake Account

The account is not Brian Armstrong. It's a fake account created to mimic his appearance. The blue checkmark could have been:
- Obtained through a paid verification service (some platforms offer this).
- Added through a browser extension that fakes checkmarks.
- Spoofed through a phishing site that mirrors Twitter.

The account has thousands of followers because bots and people fooled by the appearance follow it.

### Step 2: The DM

A few hours later, Folake gets a DM from the fake account:

Folake is excited but also hesitant. Something feels off about needing to send Bitcoin to receive Bitcoin.

But the DM is insistent, and the scammer uses authority language ("KYC step," "standard process").

## Where the Scammer Won

<Warning>
1. **Account impersonation:** The fake account looked enough like the real Brian Armstrong to pass a casual glance.
2. **Verification badge:** The checkmark (spoofed or paid) added legitimacy.
3. **Authority:** Framing it as an "official Coinbase giveaway" made it seem real.
4. **Incremental asks:** First a "verification deposit," then a "tax fee"â€”the classic fee ladder.
</Warning>

## What Folake Could Have Done (Prevention)

### Before Engaging

<Check>**Verify the account independently.** Go to Coinbase's official website and find the real CEO's Twitter handle. Compare it to the account in question.</Check>

<Check>**Check account age and tweet history.** A fake account may have inconsistent posting history or be very new.</Check>

<Check>**Never trust offers that come via DM.** Real giveaways announce winners publicly, not via DM.</Check>

<Check>**If it seems too good to be true, it is.** A CEO giving away â‚¦1M to strangers is unrealistic.</Check>

## Red Flags Checklist

<Warning>
- [ ] **Giveaway offered via DM** (not public announcement).
- [ ] **Requires sending money or crypto "upfront" for "verification."**
- [ ] **Account is newly created or has unusual posting patterns.**
- [ ] **"Tax fee," "KYC deposit," or "verification fee" required.**
</Warning>

<Steps>

## How Impersonated Influencer Scams Work

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Step 1: Account Creation" icon="user">
**What happens:** Scammer creates fake account mimicking celebrity/founder with similar name, bio, and profile picture. May obtain fake verification badge.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Step 2: Public Giveaway Post" icon="megaphone">
**What happens:** Fake account posts public giveaway announcement. Asks followers to reply with wallet address and retweet.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Step 3: DM to Winners" icon="envelope">
**What happens:** Scammer DMs victims claiming they won. Asks for "verification deposit" or "KYC fee" to claim prize.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Step 4: Fee Escalation" icon="arrow-up">
**What happens:** After first payment, scammer requests additional fees (tax, processing, etc.). Uses authority language to pressure compliance.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Step 5: Disappearance" icon="ghost">
**What happens:** Once maximum extraction achieved or victim stops paying, scammer disappears. No prize is ever delivered.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

---

## Key Learning

<Warning>
**Real giveaways do not ask you to send money first.** If you have to pay to "claim" a prize, it's a scam.
</Warning>
